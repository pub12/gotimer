Quick Answer

For most recipes at room temperature (70°F/21°C), sourdough requires **4 to 6 hours for bulk fermentation** and **2 to 3 hours for final proofing**. For best flavor, a cold retard in the fridge for **12 to 24 hours** is recommended.

Sourdough baking is a delicate dance between temperature, hydration, and time. Unlike commercial yeast, wild yeast operates on its own schedule. Understanding how to read your dough is more important than watching the clock, but having a baseline is essential for planning your day.

Key Factors Affecting Proofing

The time your sourdough takes to proof isn't just about the recipe. Several environmental variables can speed up or slow down the process significantly.

Variable Fast Proofing Slow Proofing
Temperature Warm (78°F+) Cool (65°F-)
Starter Strength Active & Peaked Slugish/Unfed
Hydration High (80%+) Low (65%-)

The Proofing Phases

1

Bulk Fermentation

This is where the magic happens. The dough develops strength and volume. Look for bubbles on the surface and a 50-75% increase in size.

2

Bench Rest & Shaping

A 20-30 minute rest after pre-shaping allows the gluten to relax, making the final tight shaping much easier.

3

Final Proof

The final rise in the banneton. This can be done at room temp (shorter) or in the fridge (longer and tastier).

The Power of Cold Retard

Cold retarding is the process of putting your shaped dough in the refrigerator (usually 38°F / 3°C) for a long period. This slows down the yeast significantly but allows the bacteria to continue producing lactic and acetic acids.

  • Enhanced Flavor: Develops that characteristic "sour" tang.
  • Better Oven Spring: Cold dough hits the hot oven and creates more steam.
  • Easier Scoring: Cold dough is firmer and easier to cut decorative patterns into.

help_center Frequently Asked Questions

Can I over-proof sourdough? expand_more

Yes. Over-proofed dough becomes weak, sticky, and will collapse in the oven. It will have a very acidic smell and won't hold its shape when scored.

How do I do the "Finger Poke Test"? expand_more

Gently poke the dough with a floured finger. If it springs back immediately, it's under-proofed. If it stays indented, it's over-proofed. If it springs back slowly and leaves a slight dimple, it's perfect.
